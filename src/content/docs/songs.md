The Songs library stores the music your church uses in worship.

Rather than keeping chord charts in a shared folder, lyrics in a separate document, and arrangements in someone's email, the Songs library brings everything into one place — connected to the gatherings where the music will be used.

## What a song can include

Each song in the library can store:

- **Title and author** — the name of the song and who wrote it
- **Lyrics** — the full text of the song, formatted for display or projection
- **Chord chart** — the chord progression and structure for musicians
- **Arrangements** — variations for different keys, team configurations, or service contexts
- **Attachments** — PDFs, audio reference files, or other supporting materials
- **Notes** — context about how your church performs the song

You do not need all of this to add a song. A title is enough to start. Details can be added as the library grows.

## Arrangements

Different teams and contexts sometimes call for the same song performed differently.

An arrangement might represent:

- A specific key (the key your team typically uses)
- An acoustic version for a smaller setting
- An extended version for a response time
- A simplified version for a different team or service

Arrangements sit under the parent song and carry their own chord chart and notes. When you add a song to a Worship Flow, you can specify which arrangement applies to that gathering.

This helps musicians prepare accurately without needing to ask which version they should use.

## Adding songs to a gathering

When you are building a Worship Flow, you can search your Songs library and insert a song directly into the flow.

The connection pulls in the relevant arrangement details, so musicians and tech team members can access the lyrics and chart from the gathering plan itself — no separate lookup needed.

## Building the library over time

You do not need a complete song library before using WorshipOS. Most teams start by adding the songs for the next service, then let the library grow naturally over several weeks.

Over time, the library becomes a historical record — which songs have been used, how often, and in what contexts. That history can inform future planning without requiring anyone to maintain a separate spreadsheet.
