Volunteers are the people who make Sunday morning happen — and Wednesday night, and kids ministry, and the welcome team at the door.

WorshipOS helps leaders coordinate that work without treating people like resources to be allocated. The goal is scheduling that protects people while ensuring the church is cared for.

## Volunteer roles

Each volunteer in WorshipOS is associated with one or more roles — the specific ways they are willing to serve.

Examples:

- Worship leader
- Vocals
- Guitar, keys, bass, drums
- Sound engineer
- Projection / slides
- Video
- Kids ministry
- Welcome and greeting
- Ushers
- Security

Roles connect to the scheduling process. When you build a Worship Flow and need to assign someone to a role, WorshipOS draws from the people associated with that role.

## Availability

Volunteers can indicate when they are available and when they are not.

This can include:

- Regular unavailability (a musician who cannot serve on first Sundays)
- One-off blocks (vacation, travel, a busy season)
- Preferred frequency (someone who wants to serve twice a month, not every week)

Availability information is there to help leaders schedule wisely. It is not a substitute for actually knowing your volunteers — but it means a leader does not have to remember every constraint from memory.

## Serving history

WorshipOS tracks a record of when each person has served and in what role.

This history is intentionally understated. It is not a score or a leaderboard. It exists so that leaders can notice patterns — someone who has served every week for months without a break, someone whose rhythm has shifted, someone who used to be deeply involved and has quietly stepped back.

Those patterns are worth noticing. What they mean is something only the leader who knows that person can judge.

## Scheduling with care

When you are assigning volunteers for a gathering, WorshipOS surfaces:

- Who is available for that date
- How recently each person has served
- Any notes relevant to that volunteer's involvement

The scheduling decision stays with the leader. The system provides the information; the person makes the call.

This matters especially in smaller churches where the same people are relied on week after week. Scheduling well means protecting those people from burnout — not just filling every slot.

## Communicating with volunteers

Once volunteers are assigned to a gathering, they can receive a notification with their role and the service details.

Volunteers can confirm their availability for a specific gathering, and leaders can see at a glance who has confirmed and who has not responded.

This replaces the back-and-forth of individual messages for routine scheduling — while keeping communication personal when the situation calls for it.
