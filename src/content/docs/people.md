People are the foundation of everything in WorshipOS.

Rather than treating congregation members as rows in a database, WorshipOS keeps individual records connected to the relationships and rhythms that matter — households, serving history, group involvement, and care.

## Individual records

Each person in WorshipOS has a record that can include:

- **Name and contact information** — email, phone, and address
- **Household** — which family unit they belong to
- **Volunteer roles** — the ministry areas they serve in
- **Groups** — small groups, ministry teams, or communities they are part of
- **Notes** — space for leaders to record pastoral context that a schedule cannot capture

You do not need complete information to add someone. A name is enough to get started. Details can be filled in over time.

## Households

Households group family members together under one record.

This matters in practical ways. When a family has multiple members serving in different ministries, the household view lets you see the whole picture at once. It also helps when following up — rather than contacting each person individually, you can see the family unit and reach out appropriately.

A household might look like:

> **The Martins**
> — David Martin (worship team, guitar)
> — Sarah Martin (kids ministry)
> — Emma Martin (youth group)

## Volunteer roles

Each person can be associated with one or more volunteer roles — the specific ways they serve at your church.

Examples:
- Worship leader
- Guitar
- Keys
- Sound
- Slides / ProPresenter
- Kids ministry
- Welcome team
- Ushers

Roles connect to the scheduling system. When you assign someone to a role in a Worship Flow, WorshipOS knows who is available, who has been scheduled recently, and who may need a rest.

## Serving history

WorshipOS tracks when each person has served and in what capacity.

This is intentionally quiet information. It is not a score or a leaderboard. It is simply a record that helps leaders notice patterns — someone who has served every week for three months, or someone who used to serve regularly but has stepped back.

Those patterns belong to leaders to interpret. The system surfaces them; discernment stays with the people who actually know the congregation.
