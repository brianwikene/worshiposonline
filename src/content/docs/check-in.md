Check-In helps churches welcome people and track who is present at a gathering.

It is not primarily a data collection exercise. It is a way for leaders to notice — who came, who has been absent for a while, who showed up for the first time — so that care can follow.

## Common uses

Check-In is used most often in:

- **Kids ministry** — parents check children in at the start of a service; a label or tag connects the child to the parent
- **Sunday services** — general attendance tracking for the congregation
- **Special events** — conferences, workshops, or one-time gatherings where knowing who attended matters
- **Volunteer attendance** — confirming which scheduled volunteers are actually present

## How it works

When a gathering begins, check-in opens for that event. Attendees can be checked in by a volunteer at the door, or in some configurations by the person themselves.

Each check-in ties back to a People record where one exists. New visitors can be entered on the spot and a record created for follow-up.

## Attendance and care

The most important thing Check-In produces is not a headcount. It is a pattern.

When someone who has attended every week for two years misses three Sundays in a row, that pattern matters — and someone should notice. When a family that went quiet reappears after a long absence, that matters too.

WorshipOS surfaces these patterns so that leaders can respond with care rather than discovering them after the fact.

The system can flag:

- Individuals who have not been seen for a defined period
- New or returning visitors who may benefit from a follow-up
- Families whose attendance has significantly changed

What happens next belongs to the leaders who know those people.

## The Posture of Check-In

Check-In is not only a workflow. It is a posture.

In a healthy ministry, software should help a church pay better attention without making the room feel mechanical. That is especially important in a no hype environment, where the goal is not to impress people with efficiency, but to support the quiet human work of shepherding.

- **Awareness** — using the system to notice the person in front of you, and to notice the patterns of those who have been absent
- **Availability** — keeping coordination calm and still, so leaders are protected from the rush of logistics and remain present to people
- **Willingness** — being ready to respond pastorally when the system surfaces a need for care

This is the point of Check-In at its best: not faster administration for its own sake, but clearer attention, steadier presence, and more faithful follow-up.

## Kids ministry check-in

For churches using Check-In in a kids ministry context, the system connects children to parent or guardian records. This is important for security — a child should only leave with the right person — and for pastoral care across the whole family.
